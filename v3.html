Obstetrics · Emergency
Eclampsia
Severe pre-eclampsia + generalised tonic-clonic seizures in a pregnant woman — caused by a failing placenta that poisons the mother's blood vessels.
REAL PATIENT
Priya. 22 years old. First pregnancy. 34 weeks.

Her husband rushes her to your PHC at 11 PM. She had two seizures at home. She arrives postictal — eyes deviated, foaming at mouth, unresponsive. BP 178/116. No IV access. No pump. Nearest hospital 60 km away.

1
Why did Priya's BP only rise after 20 weeks — not from day one?
Trophoblast invasion · spiral arteries · two phases
⌄
Normal: trophoblast invades spiral arteries in 2 phases
↓

Decidual segment
Phase 2 · 16–20 wks
Myometrial segment
↓ In Priya — Phase 2 never happened

↓
Placental ischemia begins · symptoms after 20 wks

In a healthy pregnancy, specialised cells called extravillous trophoblasts physically invade the walls of spiral arteries and convert them from narrow, high-resistance tubes into wide, low-resistance channels — like upgrading a garden hose to a pipeline. This happens in two phases. In Priya's case, Phase 2 failed. The myometrial segment of her spiral arteries stayed narrow. The placenta started starving for blood. And the clock started ticking.

Exception to know

Molar pregnancy causes pre-eclampsia before 20 weeks — the trophoblastic tissue is abnormal from conception, not dependent on Phase 2 failure.

sFLT-1 is the key villain. It acts as a decoy receptor — floating in the blood and grabbing free VEGF and PlGF before they can reach vessel walls and cause relaxation. The result is a body-wide vasoconstriction. Every blood vessel in Priya's body is squeezing harder than it should. Her BP isn't high because her heart is working harder. It's high because her pipes are narrower.

Best predictor of PIH

sFLT-1 : PlGF ratio — rising sFLT-1 with falling PlGF appears weeks before clinical symptoms. This is replacing the outdated roll-over test.

The glomerulus is not a special organ — it's a tightly wound ball of capillaries made of the same endothelial cells being destroyed everywhere else in Priya's body. Because it filters enormous volumes constantly, even tiny damage shows up immediately as measurable protein in urine. Proteinuria is the kidney screaming first.

Threshold to memorise

S.creatinine ≥ 1.1 mg/dl = end-organ damage. ALP is raised in all pregnancies because the placenta makes it — never use ALP as a liver damage marker in PIH. Use SGOT/SGPT only.

Two separate mechanisms are attacking Priya's brain simultaneously. The first: her blood-brain barrier is leaking, causing cerebral edema and tissue hypoxia. The second: at BP 178/116, the brain's autoregulatory system — which normally keeps cerebral blood flow constant — has been overwhelmed. Blood is ramming into the brain at full arterial pressure. The occipital lobe is the most vulnerable region, which is why visual symptoms appear before the seizure.

MRI pearl

PRES — Posterior Reversible Encephalopathy Syndrome. Edema concentrated in the posterior cerebral hemispheres. IOC for atypical eclampsia. Reversible in most cases if treated promptly.

You give MgSO₄. Why does it stop the seizure — but do nothing for Priya's BP?
NMDA block · membrane stabilisation · not a vasodilator
⌄

MgSO₄ works entirely in the brain. It has no action on smooth muscle in blood vessel walls. This is why you always give MgSO₄ and anti-HTN as two separate, parallel treatments — one quiets the brain, one controls the pressure. Skipping anti-HTN because "MgSO₄ is onboard" is one of the mistakes that causes intracranial haemorrhage.

Therapeutic window — narrow

4–7 mEq/L is therapeutic. Toxicity begins at 9 mEq/L. MgSO₄ is excreted entirely by the kidney — so when urine output drops, Mg accumulates silently. Monitor DTR before every maintenance dose.

Priya's heart is already under stress from the high-afterload state. If you use a pure vasodilator like hydralazine, the sudden BP drop triggers a reflex tachycardia — her heart rate shoots up, stressing it further. Labetalol blocks both alpha receptors (reduces vessel tone) and beta receptors (prevents the reflex tachycardia) simultaneously. The stepwise dosing — 20 → 40 → 80 → 80 mg with BP checks every 15 minutes — gives you control without overshoot.

Never do this

Sublingual nifedipine. Sublingual absorption is unpredictable — BP drops faster than you intended → uteroplacental ischaemia → acute fetal distress. Always oral nifedipine, never sublingual.

7
MgSO₄ toxicity — what happens step by step, and what do you do?
DTR · respiratory arrest · calcium gluconate
⌄
Therapeutic: 4–7 mEq/L — Priya's seizures stop
↓ Mg accumulates if kidneys slow down
9–10 mEq/L — Knee jerk disappears · STOP next dose
↓
12 mEq/L — Respiratory muscles paralysed · Respiratory arrest
↓
15–24 mEq/L — Cardiac conduction blocked · Cardiac arrest
↓ antidote
Ca gluconate 10 ml of 10% IV — slow push over 3 min

Magnesium and calcium compete at the same sites — neuromuscular junctions and cardiac membranes. When Mg²⁺ builds up too high, it blocks these sites completely. The first warning sign is always the knee jerk disappearing — check it before every maintenance dose. If it's gone, stop the drug. Calcium gluconate reverses toxicity by outcompeting Mg²⁺ at those same sites.

PHC survival rule

Draw calcium gluconate 10 ml of 10% into a labelled syringe before you start MgSO₄. Place it physically next to the patient. In a toxicity event you will have 60 seconds — not time to prepare.

The fetus has its own triage system. Faced with inadequate oxygen, it preferentially maintains blood flow to the brain at the expense of the kidneys and body. The kidneys produce fetal urine — which becomes amniotic fluid. When fetal kidneys shut down, amniotic fluid disappears. Oligohydramnios on ultrasound is the baby telling you it has been hypoxic for a while.

Umbilical Doppler thresholds

S/D ratio ≥ 3 → deliver at 37 wks. Absent end-diastolic flow → deliver at 33–34 wks. Reversed end-diastolic flow → deliver at 31–32 wks. Worse Doppler = earlier delivery.

Everything you give Priya tonight is damage control. The ischemic placenta is a biological factory pumping vasoconstrictors into her blood — and it will keep pumping until it is removed. Delivery is not a last resort. It is the treatment. The drugs buy time for the baby to be delivered safely and for Priya to be stabilised enough to survive the delivery.

Important fact

The placenta drives PIH independently of the fetus — which is why molar pregnancy (no fetus, just trophoblastic tissue) can still cause severe early-onset pre-eclampsia.

10
5 mistakes that get patients killed — and why each one happens
PHC traps · common errors · how to avoid
⌄
Mistake 1

IV loading dose given too fast. Inject 4 g over 15–20 min minimum. Rapid injection floods cardiac conduction with Mg²⁺ → cardiac arrest. No pump: 1 ml/min by hand. Count the seconds.

Mistake 2

Stopping MgSO₄ when urine output drops. Oliguria in PE is expected — the kidneys are vasoconstricted. You stop for lost knee jerk, not for oliguria. Low urine output means Mg is accumulating — watch DTR more carefully, but don't stop the drug.

Mistake 3

Sublingual nifedipine. Sublingual absorption is unpredictable and rapid → BP crashes → uteroplacental ischaemia → acute fetal distress. Always oral. Never sublingual. Ever.

Mistake 4

Rushing to the OT before MgSO₄ is onboard. Operating on Priya without seizure prophylaxis = intraoperative eclampsia. Loading dose first. Anti-HTN second. Then delivery planning.

Mistake 5

Sending a postpartum woman home with headache + high BP. Eclampsia happens up to 48 hours after delivery. "She delivered fine" means nothing. MgSO₄ continues for 24 hours post-delivery.

The one rule that prevents all five

Treat the postpartum period as carefully as the antepartum period. The placenta is out but the endothelial damage takes days to resolve.
