Intermediate Syndrome — check daily
⌄
Neck flexion: ask patient to lift head off pillow
Single breath count: say numbers in one breath — target ≥25
Proximal limb power: arm raise, hip flexion
Cranial nerves: ptosis, lateral gaze, facial droop
IS does NOT respond to atropine or 2-PAM. Only option: intubate and ventilate before arrest.
1
0 – 2 min
Decontamination — protect yourself first
⌄
OP absorbs through skin. Without gloves, you become the second patient in the room.
Gloves + gown before touching patient
Double gloves if liquid OP visible on skin
Remove ALL contaminated clothing — cut if needed
Bag clothes in plastic immediately
Flush skin with soap + water 10–15 min — hair and genitals included
Eye exposure: irrigate with NS 10–15 min
Tap water if NS unavailable
Get bottle label — WHO colour code tells toxicity class
Red = extremely toxic · Yellow = highly toxic · Blue = moderate
2
2 – 5 min
Airway + IV access
⌄
Left lateral position — prevent aspiration of secretions
O₂ mask 6–8 L/min — correct hypoxia before drugs
Atropine works poorly in a hypoxic patient
IV access ×2 — 18G or larger
Suction secretions from pharynx continuously if pooling
Intubate NOW if any of these present
RR >30/min or apnoeic spells
SpO₂ <90% despite O₂ / PaO₂ <60 mmHg
GCS ≤8 or no response to voice
Neck weakness or single breath count <25
RSI drug: Rocuronium. Never suxamethonium — pseudocholinesterase is inhibited, paralysis lasts hours instead of minutes.
3
5 – 10 min
Confirm cholinergic toxidrome
⌄
Not every pesticide is an OP. Atropine given to a non-cholinergic toxidrome causes harm.
MUSCARINIC signs — SLUDGE
Salivation · Lacrimation · Urination · Defecation · GI cramps · Emesis
Miosis (pinpoint pupils) · Bradycardia · Bronchospasm · Bronchorrhoea · Sweating
NICOTINIC signs
Muscle fasciculations · Weakness · Tachycardia (early)
Atropine challenge test (if no cholinesterase lab)
Give Atropine 1 mg IV. Monitor HR for 5 min.
HR increases >25% baseline OR >30 bpm → cholinergic toxicity confirmed
Negative test does not exclude OP. Treat clinical picture.

Pupils
Normal=0 · Miotic=1 · Pinpoint=2
RR
<20=0 · ≥20=1 · +cyanosis=2
HR
>60=0 · 41–60=1 · <40=2
Fasciculations
None=0 · Present=1 · Generalised=2
Consciousness + Seizures
Alert=0 · Impaired=1 · Unresponsive=2 · Seizures=+1
Score 0–3: Mild · 4–7: Moderate · 8–11: Severe. Moderate/severe → refer after stabilisation.
4
10 – 20 min
Atropine — titrate to target, not a fixed dose
⌄
Atropine competes with ACh at muscarinic receptors. You need far more than a normal cardiac dose — do not stop early out of fear.
Give loading dose — repeat every 5 min
Atropine sulphate 1–3 mg IV push
If no response after 5 min → double the dose and repeat
Children: 0.05 mg/kg IV every 5 min
Severe cases may need 50–100 mg before targets are met — this is normal
Do not underdose. Underdosing atropine is the most common cause of death.
Stop loading only when ALL 4 targets are met
SBP
>90 mmHg
Heart rate
>90 bpm
Chest
Clear on auscultation
Axilla
Dry — no sweating
Pupils dilating (mydriasis) is NOT a target. Do not stop atropine just because pupils are wide. Your targets are axilla and chest — not eyes.
Maintenance — after atropinisation
10–20% of total loading dose per hour — run for 48h (OP) or 24h (carbamate)
Example: took 20 mg to atropinise → run 2–4 mg/hr maintenance
No pump: add atropine to 100 ml NS, count drops manually
Indian IV set = 15 drops/ml · 1 mg/hr = 25 drops/min
Atropine toxicity signs — reduce dose ⌄
🧠EarlyConfusion · Agitation · Flushed dry skin
🌡️ModerateFever · Absent bowel sounds · Urinary retention
👁️SevereDelirium · Hallucinations — stop infusion 60 min, restart at 80% of previous dose
If atropine not available
Glycopyrrolate 0.1–0.2 mg IV — does not cross the blood-brain barrier, use only if no CNS symptoms present
5
Only if <1 hour
Gastric lavage — early, once, safely
⌄
Lavage removes less than 0.01% of ingested poison. The first dry aspiration removes 80% of whatever lavage can remove. Atropine saves the patient — lavage does not.
Before you start — confirm both
Patient is conscious OR airway is already secured
Never do lavage in a comatose patient without intubation first
Ingestion was less than 1 hour ago
After 2 hours — skip lavage entirely, focus on atropine
How to do it — step by step
Patient in left lateral position, head slightly down
Pass large-bore Ryle's tube (32–36 Fr adult)
First: dry aspiration — pull out contents with syringe before adding any fluid
This single step removes 80% of what lavage can ever remove. Save this as medicolegal sample.
Then instil 200–300 ml warm water or saline
Never more than 300 ml at once — large bolus pushes poison past pylorus into intestine
Drain by gravity — lower tube end below stomach level into bucket
No suction machine: funnel to fill, lower tube to drain. Syphon works fine.
Repeat 200–300 ml cycles until return fluid is clear
Total 2–3 litres usually enough. One session only — stop there.
Activated charcoal — if available and patient is alert
1 g/kg (max 50 g) mixed in 150 ml water — give orally or via Ryle's tube
Give within 1 hour of ingestion only
Do not give if vomiting, reduced consciousness, or no airway protection
NEVER USE THESE FOR LAVAGE
Potassium permanganate (KMnO₄) → causes mucosal burns and organ failure
1-litre bolus at once → pushes poison into small intestine
Repeated lavage every 4–8h → evidence shows zero added benefit
Sodium bicarbonate solution → risk of exothermic reaction in stomach
6
If indicated
Pralidoxime (2-PAM) — use only if criteria met
⌄
Evidence is conflicting. Two large RCTs show no survival benefit. Atropine is your anchor drug. 2-PAM is an adjunct at best.
Pralidoxime — only if ALL criteria present
Within 6h of ingestion + diethyl OP compound (parathion, chlorpyrifos) + severe/persistent nicotinic features
Loading: 30 mg/kg (1–2 g) in 100 ml NS over 15–30 min IV
Maintenance: 8 mg/kg/hr for 48 hours
CONTRAINDICATED in carbamate poisoning (Baygon, Sevin, propoxur)
DO NOT GIVE 2-PAM IF
Carbamate poisoning (worsens toxicity)
>6h since ingestion with dimethyl OP — AChE already aged, useless
Unknown compound — defer until identified
7
If seizures
Seizures + agitation
⌄
1st — Diazepam IV
10 mg slow IV push over 2 min. Repeat q5–10 min if needed. Max 30–40 mg in 24h.
Children: 0.2–0.3 mg/kg IV.
Status epilepticus: diazepam infusion 5–10 mg/hr.
Do NOT give phenytoin — ineffective in OP seizures
2nd — Propofol or Midazolam infusion
If seizures refractory to diazepam. Requires ventilation.
Never haloperidol · Never atracurium

UNDERSTAND
Medicine · Toxicology
Organophosphate Poisoning
OP compounds block AChE — acetylcholine accumulates at every synapse causing three simultaneous crises: muscarinic, nicotinic, and CNS. Death is from respiratory failure, not BP.

His family found him collapsed in the field, an empty bottle of Chlorofos 20 (chlorpyrifos) beside him. He'd swallowed it three hours ago. Reeking of kerosene, drowning in his own saliva, pinpoint pupils, chest rattling with wheeze — both lungs. Fasciculations visible on his forearms. GCS 10.
