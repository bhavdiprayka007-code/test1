Snake Bite
EMERGENCY PROTOCOL
ASV REACTION
Patient developing rash, urticaria, fever, hypotension, or bronchospasm during or after ASV infusion.
1
Stop now
Stop ASV infusion immediately
⌄
Stop infusion — do not remove IV line
Start fresh NS infusion via new IV set
O₂ 6–8 L/min via mask
2
Give now
Adrenaline 0.5 mg IM — first drug
⌄
Adrenaline 1:1000 solution → 0.5 ml IM into outer thigh or deltoid
Children: 0.01 mg/kg IM
Repeat every 5 min if no improvement
This is the most important drug. Give before antihistamine or steroid.
3
Then give
Chlorpheniramine + Hydrocortisone IV
⌄
Chlorpheniramine maleate 10 mg IV (children 0.2 mg/kg)
Hydrocortisone 100 mg IV — acts slowly, supportive role
4
After 15–30 min
Restart ASV slowly once stable
⌄
Once reaction settles, restart ASV at slower rate under close observation. Patient still needs ASV — the venom remains active.
NO ADRENALINE?
Salbutamol nebulisation for bronchospasm
IV fluids for hypotension — NS 500 ml bolus
Refer immediately if not stabilising
1
At scene
First aid — RIGht approach only
⌄
Most first aid done at scene makes things worse. The only safe intervention is immobilisation and transport.
Do these 3 things
R — Reassure the patient. Fear raises heart rate and spreads venom faster.
I — Immobilise the bitten limb. Splint + crepe bandage. Limb below heart level.
Pressure immobilisation (Sutherland wrap): firm bandage from bite upward — finger should slip under it
G — Get to hospital. Carry the patient — do not let them walk.
NEVER DO THESE — THEY KILL
Tourniquet → gangrene, massive venom surge on release
Cutting and suction → infection, no venom removed
Electric shock → burns, no evidence
Traditional remedies / herbs → delay, no benefit
Trying to kill the snake → most bites happen during this attempt
2
On arrival
Assess — venomous bite or dry bite?
⌄
10–80% of bites from venomous snakes are dry bites with no venom injected. Confirming envenomation before ASV prevents unnecessary reactions.
Local signs — check bite site
Fang marks: 2 puncture wounds 0.5–2 cm apart
Krait bite: marks may be invisible or very faint
Local swelling, bleeding, blistering, necrosis → viper bite
Regional lymphadenopathy → indication for ASV
No local signs does NOT mean safe — krait bites are painless and leave no swelling
Systemic signs — check these
Ptosis (drooping eyelid) — earliest neurotoxic sign
Diplopia, difficulty swallowing, speaking, or breathing
Spontaneous bleeding from gums, IV site, or bite — viper DIC
Generalised weakness, paralysis
All snake bite patients: IV access, O₂ ready, keep nil by mouth, observe minimum 24 hours even if currently asymptomatic.
3
Bedside test
20-Minute Whole Blood Clotting Test (20WBCT)
⌄
The best coagulation test you can do at a PHC with no lab. Tells you if viper venom is consuming the patient's clotting factors right now.
How to do it
Take 2–3 ml fresh venous blood in a clean, dry glass vial or test tube
Must be glass — plastic prevents clot formation and gives false positives
Leave completely undisturbed for exactly 20 minutes at room temperature
Gently tilt the vial at 20 minutes
Blood has clotted
Normal — elapid bite or dry bite
Blood still liquid
Viper envenomation — start ASV now
Repeat 20WBCT every 30 min for 3 hours, then hourly. After ASV: repeat at 6h and 12h to confirm coagulation restored.
4
If envenomation confirmed
Anti-Snake Venom (ASV) — when and how
⌄
ASV is the only specific treatment. Give only when envenomation is confirmed. ASV neutralises circulating venom only — it cannot reverse damage already done.
Start ASV if ANY of these present
Ptosis, diplopia, or any neuromuscular sign
Spontaneous bleeding from any site
20WBCT positive (blood still liquid at 20 min)
Rapidly progressive local swelling
Regional lymphadenopathy
Hypotension or shock
How to give ASV
Preparation
Reconstitute each lyophilised vial in 10 ml distilled water (liquid form: use directly)
Add 5–10 vials to 100–200 ml normal saline
No test dose needed — test dose does not predict anaphylaxis and wastes time
Initial dose
Mild envenomation: 8–10 vials IV over 1 hour (1–2 ml/min)
Severe envenomation: 10–15 vials IV — same rate
Children: same dose as adults — venom dose is not weight-dependent
No pump: 15 drops/ml set → 1–2 ml/min = 15–30 drops/min
Repeat doses
If 20WBCT still positive at 6 hours → give same dose again
If neurotoxic signs worsening → repeat dose at 1–2 hours
Maximum documented: up to 30 vials in severe cases
ASV does NOT work for

Hump-Nosed Viper (Hypnale hypnale) — polyvalent ASV is ineffective. Supportive care only. Dialysis if renal failure develops.

5
Neurotoxic bites
Neostigmine + Atropine — cobra bites only
⌄
Cobra venom blocks post-synaptic nicotinic receptors — neostigmine reverses this. Krait venom blocks pre-synaptic receptors — neostigmine has no effect.
Atropine-Neostigmine (AN) regimen
Step 1: Atropine first — always
Atropine 0.6 mg IV slow push (children 0.02 mg/kg)
Wait 2–3 minutes before giving neostigmine
Atropine prevents muscarinic side effects of neostigmine (excessive secretions, bradycardia)
Step 2: Neostigmine IV
Neostigmine 2.5 mg IV slow (children 0.04 mg/kg)
Repeat every 30 minutes — up to 3 doses
Positive response: ≥50% improvement in ptosis within 1 hour
No response after 3 doses = krait bite or venom already at pre-synaptic site — stop neostigmine
Give one AN dose before transfer to higher centre. Cobra neurotoxicity can progress rapidly during transport.
6
Respiratory watch
Single Breath Count — do every 15–20 min
⌄
Respiratory failure is the most common cause of death in neurotoxic bites. The Single Breath Count detects declining respiratory muscle power before the patient crashes.
Ask patient to take a deep breath and count aloud from 1 as far as possible
Normal: count reaches 25–30 in one breath
Count <15 → prepare for intubation now
Count <10 or tidal volume <200 ml → intubate immediately
At PHC without ventilator: bag-mask ventilation can sustain a krait bite patient for hours. Transfer immediately. Neurotoxic paralysis from krait bites is fully reversible if the patient survives the respiratory failure.
7
Haematotoxic bites
Bleeding · DIC · AKI — viper envenomation
⌄
Russell's viper and Saw-scaled viper venom consumes all clotting factors. The patient bleeds from everywhere — gums, IV site, bite wound, urine. ASV is the only cure. Everything else is buying time.
Bleeding management
Give adequate ASV first — do not transfuse before ASV
Transfusing clotting factors while venom is still active = replacing factors directly into venom. Waste.
Active severe bleeding after adequate ASV → Fresh Frozen Plasma (FFP) 4–6 units
No FFP available → Fresh whole blood transfusion
Strict bed rest — even minor trauma will cause haematoma in DIC
No NSAIDs, no aspirin, no IM injections until coagulopathy corrected
Tetanus toxoid only after coagulopathy is corrected
Injecting during active DIC causes massive haematoma at injection site
Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) — Russell's viper only
Target urine output 1 ml/kg/hour — monitor strictly
If oliguria: IV fluids 200 ml NS bolus → reassess → repeat
Dialysis indications: K⁺ >6.5 · Urea >300 · Anuria · Severe acidosis · Fluid overload
Any single criterion is sufficient — do not wait for all five
Forced Alkaline Diuresis (FAD) if dialysis not available
500 ml NS + 50 ml 8.4% NaHCO₃ + 100 ml 20% Mannitol over 1 hour · repeat cycles · monitor urine output hourly
Pregnancy — snakebite in pregnant women
ASV: same dose as non-pregnant adults — do not reduce
The venom dose is the same. Undertreating risks both mother and foetus.
ASV is safe in pregnancy — benefit far outweighs any theoretical risk
Refer to gynaecologist for foetal assessment after stabilisation
Assess foetal heart rate, signs of placental abruption
DIC especially dangerous in pregnancy — risk of PPH, placental abruption
Compartment syndrome
Signs: tense swelling, pain on passive stretch, absent distal pulse, paraesthesia
Do NOT incise at PHC/CHC level — fasciotomy requires surgical facility + corrected coagulopathy
Give ASV, elevate limb, refer urgently for compartment pressure measurement
8
Investigations
Labs — what to send, why, and when
⌄
Labs confirm what clinical signs suggest — they do not replace them. Never delay ASV for lab results if clinical envenomation is clear.
All levels — do immediately
20WBCT — the most important bedside test
Glass vial only · 2 ml blood · 20 min undisturbed
BP, Pulse, RR, SpO₂, Temperature every 15 min in active envenomation
Single Breath Count every 15–20 min in neurotoxic bites
Urine — observe colour at every void (pink/red/brown = haemoglobinuria/myoglobinuria)
District hospital — send on admission
CBC + Platelet count
Thrombocytopenia → viper · Neutrophilia → systemic absorption
PT / APTT
Prolonged → viper coagulopathy · Repeat 6-hourly until normal
Serum Creatinine + BUN
Rising → AKI (Russell's viper). Baseline then daily.
Serum Electrolytes
K⁺ >6.5 → emergency dialysis trigger
Blood Group + Rh
Before FFP or blood transfusion
Urine dipstick + microscopy
Haematuria · haemoglobinuria · casts → AKI
Tertiary / ICU — add these
Fibrinogen + D-Dimer + FDP
Low fibrinogen + high D-Dimer = VICC confirmed
LFT — SGOT / SGPT
Hepatic injury. Note: ALP unreliable — liver disease elevates it too.
CPK + Urine Myoglobin
Myotoxicity (sea snake, Russell's). CPK >1000 → rhabdomyolysis.
ABG (arterial)
Neurotoxic bites — CO₂ retention = impending failure. Caution: do NOT do arterial puncture in active coagulopathy
ECG
Sinus tachycardia, ST changes, AV block all reported in viper bites
Peripheral smear
Schistocytes (helmet cells) → microangiopathic haemolysis in VICC
Arterial puncture is contraindicated in patients with haemostatic abnormalities — risk of uncontrollable bleeding at puncture site. Use venous blood gas or SpO₂ + clinical assessment instead.
9
All bites
Supportive care + adjuncts
⌄
Tetanus toxoid IM — only after ruling out or correcting coagulopathy
Antibiotics if local cellulitis or necrosis — Amoxyclav 625 mg BD oral or 1.2 g IV 8-hourly
Pain: paracetamol or tramadol only
No NSAIDs, no aspirin — worsen bleeding in viper bites
No corticosteroids, no heparin, no antihistamines as treatment — ineffective for envenomation
Observe minimum 24 hours — even asymptomatic patients, even if snake appears non-venomous
10
Before transfer
Referral checklist + discharge criteria
⌄
Complete before shifting patient
IV access secured, O₂ running
20WBCT done and result documented
ASV first dose given (if envenomation confirmed)
Document: number of vials, time given, any reaction
Single breath count checked and documented
One AN dose given if cobra neurotoxicity present
Tetanus toxoid given
Receiving facility called and briefed

Snake Bite
64,000 deaths annually in India. The bite that arrives quietly, kills overnight, and responds completely to a drug that costs ₹200 per vial — if you give it in time.
REAL PATIENT
Bhikhabhai. 52 years old. Farmer. Found by his wife at 5 AM.

He had gone to irrigate his field at midnight. Came home, said something bit his foot, went to sleep. At 5 AM his wife couldn't wake him. No swelling. No bite mark visible. No pain. By the time she brought him to the PHC — he was apnoeic. Common krait. Bit him at midnight. Killed him silently while he slept.
